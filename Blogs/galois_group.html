Galois Groups and Inverse Limits

February 12, 2026

Recall that a ${\color{red} Galois extension}$ is a normal and separable field extension. The Galois theory of finite Galois extensions $L/K$ tells us there is a one-to-one correspondence between

$$ \begin{array}{ccc} \{\text{intermediate fields of } L/K\} & \longleftrightarrow & \{\text{subgroups of } \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\} \\ M & \longleftrightarrow & \mathrm{Gal}(L/M) \\ \cup & & \cap \\ L^H & \longleftrightarrow & H \end{array} $$

such that

$$ [M:K] = [\Gal(L/K):\Gal(L/M)] \tag{1.1} $$

This means to study the intermediate fields of $L/K$ it suffices to study subgroups of $\Gal(L/K)$. This turns out to be false for infinite Galois extensions! Here is one example.

Example. Consider the field $$ K = \Q\lb\sqrt d: d\in\Z\text{ is squarefree}\rb = \Q(\sqrt{-1},\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3},\sqrt{5},\cdots) $$ This field $K$ is essentially the composite field of all quadratic extensions over $\Q$ (every quadratic extension over $\Q$ looks like $\Q(\sqrt{d})$ for $d$ squarefree). Since each $\Q(\sqrt{d})$ is a normal extension over $\Q$ we know $K$ is normal over $\Q$. Hence $K/\Q$ is a Galois extension. The action of $\sigma\in\Gal(K/\Q)$ sends each $\sqrt{d}$ to $\pm\sqrt{d}$, so it turns out that $$ G := \Gal(K/\Q) \cong \prod_{i\in I} \{\pm 1\} \cong \prod_{n=1}^\infty \{\pm 1\} $$ where $I = \{-1,2,3,5,\cdots\}$. There are only countably many such quadratic extensions of $\Q$ and they are all contained in $K$. By the formula (1.1) we ``should'' have a bijective correspondence $$ \begin{array}{ccc} \{\text{deg 2 intermediate fields of } K/\Q\} & \longleftrightarrow & \{\text{index 2 subgroups of } \mathrm{Gal}(K/\Q)\} \end{array} $$ We know the LHS is a countable set. However, it turns out that there are uncountably many subgroups of $G$ of index 2! This is a contradiction :( \qed

This tells us the correspondence for finite Galois extensions is no long true for infinite Galois extensions. As always, topology comes in handy in the infinite setting. For an infinite Galois extension $L/K$ we can define a topology on $\Gal(L/K)$ such that there is a new one-to-one correspondence

$$ \begin{array}{ccc} \{\text{intermediate fields of } L/K\} & \longleftrightarrow & \{\text{closed subgroups of } \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\} \\ M & \longleftrightarrow & \mathrm{Gal}(L/M) \\ \cup & & \cap \\ L^H & \longleftrightarrow & H \end{array} $$

Now the question is: how do we put a topology on $\Gal(L/K)$??? For motivation, we have to view $\Gal(L/K)$ in a different way. A general fact from Galois theory says for a Galois extension $L/K$

$$ L = \bigcup_{\substack{K\subseteq M\subseteq L\\ M/K \text{ finite Galois}}} M \tag{1.2} $$

is the union of all finite Galois subextensions $M$ of $K$. What is $\Gal(L/K)$? It is just the set of all automorphisms $\sigma:L\to L$ such that $\sigma\vert_K = \mr{id}_K$. How can we define such an automorphism? For a $\sigma\in\Gal(L/K)$ and $L/M/K$ finite, the restriction $\sigma_M = \sigma\vert_M$ turns out to be an element of $\Gal(M/K)$.

Can we do this in the reverse direction? Since $L$ is the union of finite extensions by (1.2), it suggests that we can define a $\sigma\in\Gal(L/K)$ ``gluing'' a collection of $\sigma_M\in \Gal(M/K)$. Let $\sigma_M\in\Gal(M/K)$ for each $L/M/K$ finite. It is tempting to define

$$ \sigma: L\to L\ \text{ by }\ \sigma(x) = \sigma_M(x)\ \text{ if $x\in M\subseteq L$ finite Galois over $K$} $$

This definition depends on the choice of $M$ that $x$ lies in. Suppose $x\in M_1$ as well, then what if $\sigma_M(x) \neq \sigma_{M_1}(x)$? This suggests we should impose conditions on this collection of $\sigma_M$'s. How can we do it? It is simple, we can just require

$$ \sigma_M(x) = \sigma_{M_1}(x)\ \text{ for all $x\in M\cap M_1$} $$

Equivalantly, it is the same as requiring $\sigma_M\vert_N = \sigma_N$ for all $N\subseteq M\subseteq L$ finite Galois extensions over $K$. This motivates the following definition.

Definition. Let $L/K$ be a Galois extension. We define $$ \mr{GAL}(L/K) := \left\{(\sigma_M)\in\prod_{\substack{K\subseteq M\subseteq L\\ M/K \text{ finite Galois}}} \Gal(M/K) : \sigma_M\vert_N = \sigma_N \text{ if $K\subseteq N\subseteq M\subseteq L$}\right\} $$
Remark. This is a subgroup of the product $\prod \Gal(M/K)$, where the group operation is component-wise. We claim that this is the same as our usual Galois group $\Gal(L/K)$.
Theorem. Let $L/K$ be a Galois extension. Then $$ \phi: \Gal(L/K) \to \mr{GAL}(L/K) $$ by $\phi(\sigma) = (\sigma\vert_M)_M$ is a group homomorphism with inverse $$ \psi: \mr{GAL}(L/K) \to \Gal(L/K) $$ by defining $\psi((\sigma_M)_M)$ to be the automorphism $\sigma:L\to L$ by $\sigma(x) = \sigma_M(x)$ if $x\in M$. It then follows that $$ \Gal(L/K) \cong \mr{GAL}(L/K)\ \text{ as groups} $$
It is easy. Just check definitions.

Now we have identified $\Gal(L/K)$ with $\mr{GAL}(L/K)$, we might abuse notation and use both interpretation when we talk about the usual Galois group $\Gal(L/K)$.

Why is this definition of $\Gal(L/K)$ useful? It is because $\Gal(L/K)$ is a subgroup of the product $\prod \Gal(M/K)$. Each $\Gal(M/K)$ is a finite set with the discrete topology. Therefore $\prod \Gal(M/K)$ has a product topology: this is the smallest topology such that the projection

$$ \pi_M: \prod \Gal(M/K) \to \Gal(N/K) $$

is continuous for all $L/N/K$ finite Galois. Hence, as a subset of $\prod \Gal(M/K)$, we can give $\Gal(L/K)$ the subspace topology! This is a very natural topology on $\Gal(L/K)$ using this interpretation. We call this topology the $\defn{Krull topology}$.

Since each $\Gal(M/K)$ has discrete topology, it is compact. Hence the product $\prod \Gal(M/K)$ is compact by Tychonoff Theorem. It turns out that $\Gal(L/K)$ is a closed subset of $\prod \Gal(M/K)$, we can conclude that

Theorem. $\Gal(L/K)$ is compact.
Construction (Inverse Limit). This interpretation of Galois group as a subset of a product is a special case of a general construction called the $\defn{inverse limit}$. This construction allows us to ``glue small objects to a big object''. Let $(I,\le)$ be a poset. For each $i\in I$ assume there is a set $A_i$ and for all $i,j\in I$ with $i\ge j$ suppose there is a map $$ \varphi_{i,j}: A_i \to A_j $$ The inverse limit of $(A_i, \varphi_{i,j})$ is defined as $$ \varprojlim_{i\in I} A_i := \left\{(a_i)_{i\in I} \in \prod_{i\in I} A_i : \varphi_{i,j}(a_i) = a_j\ \text{ for $i\ge j$ in $I$}\right\} $$ In the case of Galois groups, we have $I = \{M: K\subseteq M\subseteq L \text{ and $M/K$ is finite Galois}\}$ and the map $\varphi_{M,N}(\sigma) = \sigma\vert_N$ for $N\subseteq M$. Hence we can write $$ \Gal(L/K) = \varprojlim_{\substack{K\subseteq M\subseteq L\\ M/K \text{ finite Galois}}} \Gal(M/K) $$

Another common construction using inverse limit is the $p$-adic numbers.

What does this topology look like? For example, what are the open sets? The following lemma turns out to be very useful.

Lemma. Let $\sigma\in\Gal(L/K)$. Then $\sigma\Gal(L/M)$ is an open neighborhood of $\sigma$ for all $M/K$ finite Galois extensions.
Since $\mr{id}_L\in\Gal(L/M)$ it follows that $\sigma\in\sigma\Gal(L/M)$. Hence it suffices to check $\sigma\Gal(L/M)$ is an open set. This is easy. By the definition of product topology we know $$ \pi_M: \Gal(L/K) \to \Gal(M/K) $$ is continuous. Note that $\sigma\in\Gal(L/K)$ so $\sigma\vert_M\in\Gal(M/K)$ and $\{\sigma\vert_M\}$ is an open set in $\Gal(M/K)$. Hence $\pi_M^{-1}(\sigma\vert_M)$ is open in $\Gal(L/K)$. We claim that $$ \pi_M^{-1}(\sigma\vert_M) = \sigma\Gal(L/M) $$ Let $\tau\in\sigma\Gal(L/M)$ so $\tau = \sigma\sigma'$ for some $\sigma'\in\Gal(L/M)$. Then $\sigma'\vert_M = \mr{id}_M$ so that $\pi(\tau) = \pi(\sigma) = \sigma\vert_M$. Therefore $\sigma\Gal(L/M)\subseteq \pi_M^{-1}(\sigma\vert_M)$. On the other hand let $\tau\in\pi_M^{-1}$ so that $\tau\vert_M = \pi_M(\tau) = \sigma\vert_M$. This means $(\tau\sigma^{-1})\vert_M = \mr{id}_M$ and so $\tau\sigma^{-1}\in\Gal(L/M)$. Therefore $\tau\in\sigma\Gal(L/M)$ so that $\pi_M^{-1}(\sigma\vert_M) = \sigma\Gal(L/M)$. This proved that $\sigma\Gal(L/M)$ is open as $\pi_M^{-1}(\sigma\vert_M)$ is open.
Remark. This lemma tells us that for each $\sigma\in\Gal(L/K)$ we have a basis of open neighborhoods of $\sigma$ $$ \{\sigma\Gal(L/M): M/K \text{ is finite Galois}\} $$ The bigger the extension $M/K$ is, the smaller the neighborhood $\sigma\Gal(L/M)$. Let $\tau\in\Gal(L/K)$ be another automorphism. Using the Krull topology how do we measure if $\tau$ and $\sigma$ are ``close''? $\tau$ and $\sigma$ are ``close'' if and only if $\tau$ lies in a small neighborhood of $\sigma$, which means $\tau\in\sigma\Gal(L/M)$ for some big finite Galois extension $M/K$. And $\tau\in\sigma\Gal(L/M)$ means $\tau\vert_M = \sigma\vert_M$. This explains the Krull topology: $$ \text{$\sigma$ and $\tau$ are ``close'' $\iff$ they agree on some ``big'' finite Galois extension $M/K$} $$

After defining the Krull topology we are finally in the right place to define Galois representations.

Definition. Let $L/K$ be a Galois extension. A $\defn{Galois representation}$ is a continuous homomorphism $$ \rho: \Gal(L/K) \to \mr{GL}(V) \cong \mr{GL}_n(k) $$ for an $n$-dimensional vector space $V$ over a field $k$ (where $k$ has a topology).
Remark. Unlike the representation of finite groups, we require this homomorphism to be continuous. This is only possible because we have a topology on the group. The reason we need continuity will be discussed later in the notes.
Example. Now we present a very important $1$-dimensional Galois representation, called the $\l$-adic cyclotomic character. Consider the Galois extension $$ \Q(\zeta_{\l^\infty}) := \Q(\zeta_{\l^n}: n\in\N) $$ the composite field of all $\l$-power-cyclotomic extension and $\zeta_{\l^n} := e^{2\pi i/\l^n}$. For each $n\ge 1$ we have a group isomorphism $$ \Gal(\Q(\zeta_{\l^n})/\Q) \to (\Z/\l^n\Z)^\times\ \text{ by }\ \sigma\mapsto a\ \text{ where $\sigma(\zeta_{\l^n}) = \zeta_{\l^n}^a$} $$ By taking the inverse limit, we claim that we get a group isomorphism \begin{align*} \Gal(\Q(\zeta_{\l^\infty})/\Q) = \varprojlim_{n} \Gal(\Q(\zeta_{\l^n})/\Q) &\longrightarrow \varprojlim_n (\Z/\l^n\Z)^\times = \Z_\l^\times\\ (\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_3, \cdots) &\mapsto (a_1, a_2, a_3\cdots) \end{align*} where $\sigma_n\in\Gal(\Q(\zeta_{\l^n})/\Q)$ and $\sigma_n(\zeta_{\l^n}) = \zeta_{\l^n}^{a_n}$. We need this is well-defined by checking $(a_1, a_2, a_3\cdots)$ is an element of $\Z_\l^\times$. Wwe need to check $a_{n+1} = a_{n}\mod{\l^n}$ for $n\ge 1$. Since $(\sigma_n)\in\Gal(\Q(\zeta_{\l^\infty})/\Q)$ we know $\sigma_{n+1}\vert_{\Q(\zeta_{\l^n})} = \sigma_n$. Hence $$ \zeta_{\l^{n}}^{a_{n}} = \sigma_{n+1}(\zeta_{\l^{n}}) = \sigma_{n+1}(\zeta_{\l^{n+1}}^\l) = \sigma_{n+1}(\zeta_{\l^{n+1}})^\l = \zeta_{\l^{n+1}}^{\l a_{n+1}} = \zeta_{\l^n}^{a_{n+1}} $$ Since $\zeta_{\l^n}$ is the primitive $\l^n$-th root of unity, $\zeta_{\l^{n}}^{a_{n}} = \zeta_{\l^n}^{a_{n+1}}$ implies $a_{n+1} = a_n\mod{\l^n}$. It follows that $\Gal(\Q(\zeta_{\l^n})/\Q) \to \Z_\l^\times$ is well-defined. The fact that this map is a group isomorphism is easy: it comes directly from the fact that each $\Gal(\Q(\zeta_{\l^n})/\Q) \to (\Z/\l^n\Z)^\times$ is a group isomorphism. This then gives a map $$ \begin{array}{ccccccc} \chi_\ell: G_{\mathbb Q} & \twoheadrightarrow & \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb Q(\zeta_{\ell^\infty})/\mathbb Q) & \overset{\sim}{\longrightarrow} & \mathbb Z_\ell^\times & \hookrightarrow & \mathbb Q_\ell^\times = \mathrm{GL}_1(\mathbb Q_\ell) \end{array} $$ which defines a 1-dimensional representation of $G_\Q$ over the field $\Q_\l$, the desired $\l$-adic cyclotomic character. Let's check this is continuous.
